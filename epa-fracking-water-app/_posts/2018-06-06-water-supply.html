Public Supply of Water

Domestic water use is water used for indoor and outdoor household purposes. Common indoor uses include water for drinking, food preparation, and bathing, washing clothes and dishes, and flushing toilets. Major outdoor uses include watering lawns and gardens and washing cars. Water for domestic use may be delivered from a public supplier or may be self-supplied. Self-supplied users may withdraw water from a private source, such as a well or spring, or capture rainwater in a cistern.

The 64 counties of Colorado are designated as Urban, Semi-Urban (we have combined these two for analytical purposes), Rural and Frontier. (Source: Colorado Counties Designation)

Analysis suggests that around 81% of total public supply is for domestic uses.(Source: Water Use for Colorado, Data for the years 2000, 2005 and 2010 is missing.)

Regression Model

We built a predictive model to:

  1. Predict conveyance losses using the variables available
  2. To see if efficient irrigation methods help minimize these losses

Results

  1. The model has R 2 of 0.96 which means 96% of the variability in the target or dependent variable is explained by the independent variables.
  2. The variable ‘Total sprinkler irrigation, in thousand acres’ has a negative coefficient. This means that for an increase in acres of irrigation by sprinklers, there is a decrease in conveyance loss. This suggests that efficient irrigation methods could minimize conveyance losses.
Regression Equation

Total Conveyance loss, in Mgal/d =

  • Total self-supplied groundwater withdrawals, in Mgal/d * 0.4524
  • + Total self-supplied surface-water withdrawals, in Mgal/d * 0.3773
  • + Total sprinkler irrigation, in thousand acres * -0.4019
  • + Total surface irrigation, in thousand acres * -0.2908 + -2.164
